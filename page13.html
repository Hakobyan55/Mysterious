CATHEDRAL OF THE HOLY MOTHER OF GOD

Cathedral of the Holy Mother of God (Yot Verk) Armenian Name: Սուրբ Աստվածածին Մայր Տաճար Alternate Name: Yot Verk ("Seven Wounds") Location: Vartanants Square, Gyumri, Shirak Province, Armenia Denomination: Armenian Apostolic Church Construction Period: 1873–1884 Diocese: Seat of the Diocese of Shirak Architectural Description General Style Style: Traditional Armenian ecclesiastical architecture Material: Tuff stone (common in Armenian churches) Form: Cruciform interior with a rectangular outer shape Domes: One large central dome and two smaller domes flanking it, symbolizing the Holy Trinity. Bell Tower A belfry rises above the main western entrance. It is tall and graceful, adding vertical emphasis to the structure. The bell tower is often used to announce masses and special ceremonies. Interior The interior is decorated with frescoes, carved stone, and wooden elements. The lighting is mystical and soft, creating a sacred, contemplative atmosphere. At the altar is the famous icon of the Holy Mother of God with seven wounds—representing seven sufferings (sorrows) of the Virgin Mary. Why "Yot Verk"? Yot Verk translates to “Seven Wounds” or “Seven Sorrows.” The name refers to seven symbolic wounds or griefs endured by the Virgin Mary: The Prophecy of Simeon The Flight into Egypt Loss of the Child Jesus in the Temple The Way to Calvary The Crucifixion The Body of Jesus taken down from the Cross The Burial of Jesus An icon called “Seven Wounds” by artist Ghukas Avetaranich hangs inside and is venerated by thousands of pilgrims annually. Location & Setting Located in the heart of Gyumri, facing the iconic Vartanants Square. Surrounded by: City Hall Statue of Vartan Mamikonian Cafés, museums, and 19th-century buildings The cathedral is a central point during city-wide religious festivals and public commemorations. Cultural and Social Importance Spiritual Center: Main cathedral of the Shirak Diocese. Active Church: Hosts baptisms, weddings, and daily liturgies. Community Symbol: A symbol of resilience, especially after surviving the 1988 earthquake. Pilgrimage Site: People from across Armenia come to pray before the “Seven Wounds” icon for healing and intercession. Art & Iconography The iconostasis and many icons inside the church depict saints, Christ, and the Virgin Mary in Armenian style. Murals and carvings show scenes from the life of Christ and Mary. The “Seven Wounds” icon is not just art—it’s an object of deep faith and emotion for many Armenians. Visiting the Cathedral Open to the public daily, often from morning until evening. No entrance fee Respectful attire is expected. Visitors can light candles, attend liturgies, or sit quietly in prayer or reflection. Photography is allowed, but discretion is advised during services. Fun Facts During the Soviet era, when religion was suppressed, this cathedral stayed open, making it a rare exception in Soviet Armenia. It's often called the “heart of Gyumri,” just like the Holy Etchmiadzin is the heart of the Armenian Church. It survived earthquakes, wars, and regime changes, and still serves the faithful. Design: The structure follows a cruciform style with an external rectangular shape. Domes: It features a central large dome flanked by two smaller domes, emphasizing the architectural symmetry. Belfry: A bell tower is positioned atop the main western entrance, adding to the cathedral's grandeur. ​ Interior and Iconography Atar: The cathedral's altar is notable for its multi-iconic decoration, distinguishing it from other Armenian churches. Icon of the Seven Wounds: A revered icon depicting the Seven Sorrows of the Blessed Virgin Mary, crafted by Ghukas Avetaranich, is housed within the cathedral. These have since been replaced, with the original domes preserved in the churchyard as historical artifacts. ​ Cultural and Religious Importance Diocesan Seat: The cathedral serves as the seat of the Diocese of Shirak of the Armenian Apostolic Church, underscoring its ecclesiastical prominence.
