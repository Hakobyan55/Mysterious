Name: Sarnaghbyur (Սառնաղբյուր in Armenian) Meaning: The name “Sarnaghbyur” translates to “cold spring” in Armenian, likely referring to the presence of fresh, cold water sources in the area. Country: Armenia Province (Marz): Shirak Municipality: Artik Coordinates: Approximately 40.5667° N latitude, 43.8167° E longitude Elevation: About 1,950 meters (6,400 feet) above sea level Population: Around 3,500 people as of the most recent estimates (population numbers may vary slightly with time) Geography & Climate: Terrain: Sarnaghbyur is located on the northwestern part of the Armenian Highlands. It lies near the slopes of the Aragats Mountain and is surrounded by volcanic hills and plains. Climate: The village experiences a cold continental climate, with long, snowy winters and cool to warm summers. Temperatures can drop below -20°C in winter. Water Sources: The area is known for its clean springs and mountain streams, which are important for both drinking and irrigation. History & Culture: Historical Significance: Sarnaghbyur has a long history, with archaeological evidence indicating human presence in the area since ancient times. Like much of Shirak, it has preserved many traditions, songs, and customs. Churches & Heritage Sites: The Church of Surb Astvatsatsin (Holy Mother of God) is a key historical structure, dating back to the 19th century, though older religious and cultural ruins can be found nearby. Folklore: The region is rich in Armenian folklore, with legends related to its springs and surrounding hills. Traditional music and dance are part of village life. Some younger residents commute to Artik or Gyumri for work or education. Crafts: Traditional Armenian carpet weaving and embroidery are still practiced by some families. Education & Infrastructure: Schools: The village has at least one secondary school serving the local children. Healthcare: There’s a small medical center or clinic providing basic services. Roads & Access: Roads connect Sarnaghbyur to nearby towns such as Artik (the municipal center) and Gyumri, Armenia's second-largest city. Transport is available via mini-buses or personal vehicles. Utilities: Most homes have access to water, electricity, and internet, though infrastructure may be more basic than in urban areas. Nature & Surroundings: Flora and Fauna: The nearby fields and hills are home to diverse wildflowers, birds, and small mammals. Nearby Attractions: Mount Aragats (to the south) is visible from parts of the village and can be a hiking destination. Gyumri and Artik are close enough for cultural trips. Interesting Facts: During the Soviet period, Sarnaghbyur was part of a collective farming system (kolkhoz), and some of the old structures and farming equipment still remain. Here's a detailed overview of Sarnaghbyur, a village located in the Shirak Province (Marz) of Armenia: Location and Geography Sarnaghbyur (Սառնաղբյուր) is a village situated in the northwestern part of Armenia, within the Shirak Province, one of the coldest regions in the country. The village is located about 10 kilometers southeast of Gyumri, the second-largest city in Armenia, and the provincial capital of Shirak. It sits at an elevation of approximately 1,700 meters (5,577 feet) above sea level. The terrain is mountainous and hilly, typical of the Shirak Plateau, with vast open fields and a continental highland climate — cold, snowy winters and mild summers. Name and Meaning “Sarnaghbyur” literally translates from Armenian as “Cold Spring” (սառը = sar = cold, աղբյուր = aghbyur = spring/fountain), referencing the natural cold water springs in the area. The name reflects the village’s fresh, clean water sources, which are important to its identity and agriculture. Population and Demographics As of the 2011 Armenian Census, the population was approximately 2,000 residents. Most of the villagers are ethnic Armenians, and the predominant language is Armenian. The majority of the population is Christian, primarily followers of the Armenian Apostolic Church. History and Heritage Sarnaghbyur has ancient roots, with historical connections dating back centuries. The area has been inhabited since early Armenian history. The village is near regions affected by major historical events like the Armenian Genocide, World War I, and the Soviet era. It was part of the Armenian SSR during the Soviet Union, and agriculture was collectivized during that time. After Armenia gained independence in 1991, the village remained largely rural but saw some development. Churches and Cultural Sites The St. Sarkis Church (Սուրբ Սարգիս եկեղեցի), built in the 19th century, is one of the notable religious landmarks. There are traditional Armenian khachkars (cross-stones) and cemeteries in and around the village, reflecting centuries of Christian heritage. Economy and Livelihood The economy is primarily based on agriculture and animal husbandry. People grow wheat, barley, potatoes, and keep cattle and sheep. Due to the region’s fertile soil and cold springs, Sarnaghbyur is good for crop cultivation and pasture-based farming. Many residents also work in Gyumri or even abroad, sending remittances back home.
